Spontaneous wake-ups (fixed)

When I started troubleshooting the suspend issue, I stumbled on the firmware update from Lenovo

Tip: S3 and other ACPI power states

There are 5 main ACPI power states defined.

SDescription[3]
S0The computer is running, CPU is online
S0ixPartial processor SoC sleep
S1Most devices are powered off. CPUs and RAM remain powered
S2CPUs are powered-off. Many functions are off-loaded to the firmware (BIOS)
S3RAM refresh rate reduces
S4RAM state is dumped to disk. RAM powered off
S5Everything is off except the power button

S0ix was most recently introduced by Intel and offered lower latency when transitioning back to S0. But its energy consumption is slightly higher than of S3[4].

Another essential difference between the two is that in S3, the OS kernel dumps its state and passes the control to the firmware (BIOS), and in S0ix, the OS kernel keeps managing all power-related functions even when the system is "asleep". S0ix requires support from the OS kernel, and it sometimes may be missing some combination of kernels and platforms.

In Lenovo firmware, there is an option Sleep state with the choice of 2 values WindowsÂ 10 and Linux.

However, both values have no direct relation to either Windows 10 (or any other version for that matter) or Linux but represent S0ix and S3 correspondingly.

So the issue was caused by a bug in Lenovo firmware.

Note: Whatâ€™s an e-cert anyway?

Multiple times MarkRHPearson mentioned some e-cert, energy-cert, e-certification, etc. Eventually, he revealed that e-cert is the Energy Star certification.

Energy Star is a US government-backed certification program for various appliances and electronics. The focus is not on the product quality but on how much energy consumers can save if they use a certified product.

The document posted by MarkRHPearson describes program requirements for "Desktop, Integrated Desktop, and Notebook Computers", and the tricky part, is that these three fall into the same category.

Conclusion

I thought it through: the two things I liked about ThinkPads the most are their unique ergonomics and the above-average Linux support.

Most of the ThinkPads' unique features, as Iâ€™ve realized upon writing this article, are long gone. The only remaining part of the legendary ThinkPad ergonomics is its TrackPoint. The Linux support is still slightly above average, although the average, thanks to the overall progress and companies like Framework computer and System76 have increased. But there are still plenty of vendors out there who continue making questionable technical decisions hurting Linux support.
